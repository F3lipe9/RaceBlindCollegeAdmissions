ü§î Wait, what actually happened?

You've probably heard about the Supreme Court ruling on college admissions. It can sound scary, but let's break it down simply.

STOP

Colleges can NO LONGER use race by itself as a deciding factor.

GO

Colleges CAN AND DO want to understand your background, experiences, and perspective as part of your whole story.

The bottom line: "Race-blind" doesn't mean "you-blind." Your identity matters. Now, it's all about how you connect it to your journey.

The Power of Your Words (The Essay)

This is your microphone. Use your personal statement and short answers to talk about your background, culture, or challenges you've faced. The key is to connect these to your character, your goals, and what you'll bring to campus.

üí° Pro Tip: Shift from "I am [X]" to "Being [X] taught me [Y], which is why I care deeply about [Z]."

The Power of Your Impact (Activities & Context)

It's not just what you did, but why it mattered. Did you work to support your family? Care for siblings? Translate for your community? Start a club to address a problem? These aren't just tasks‚Äîthey're evidence of responsibility, resilience, and leadership.

üí° Pro Tip: Frame every activity to show contribution and growth.

The Power of Your People (Recommendations)

Choose recommenders who know your story. Ask teachers, counselors, or mentors who can speak to your character and how you've overcome obstacles or contributed uniquely. A great letter adds depth to your narrative.

üìù How to Write About Your Background

This is the most important part of your toolkit. Here's how to approach it.

1

Show, Don't Just Tell

Use a specific, vivid story or moment to illustrate your point. Instead of saying "I'm resilient," describe the time you navigated a specific challenge and what you learned.

2

Connect to Core Values

Link your experience to traits like curiosity, empathy, determination, or justice. How has your background shaped what you value?

3

Look Forward

How will your perspective shape your future goals and contributions in college? Connect your past experiences to what you hope to achieve.

üîç You're Choosing Them, Too.

A college's response to this ruling tells you what they value. Come to your tours and interviews armed with questions.

"How do you train your admissions officers to read applications holistically?"

"Can you share examples of how students from diverse backgrounds have enriched campus life here?"

"What support systems exist for first-generation or low-income students?"

Asking these questions shifts your role from a passive applicant to an informed advocate for your own education.
